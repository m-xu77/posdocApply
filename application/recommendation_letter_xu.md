Dear Members of the Search Committee,

I write in strong support of Mengnan Xu's application for the Postdoctoral Fellowship
at the Centre on Contemporary China and the World. I have known Mengnan since 2016,
when I served as her co-supervisor during her Master of Public Management studies at
Beijing Normal University's School of Social Development and Public Policy. Our
relationship has continued through her doctoral research and through collaborative
empirical work in which we have been joint authors. I am glad to recommend her to
CCCW without reservation.

As Mengnan's co-supervisor, I observed at close range the qualities that distinguish
her from most graduate students I have worked with. She demonstrated throughout her
master's studies a rare capacity for independent critical thinking — she did not
merely synthesize existing frameworks but interrogated their assumptions and pushed
toward her own analytical positions. Her master's thesis reflected this independence:
it was a substantive piece of work that I regarded, and still regard, as above the
standard typically expected at that level. On the strength of her performance, her
primary supervisor Professor Xiulan Zhang invited her to continue directly into the
doctoral program — a judgment I fully endorsed.

My direct research collaboration with Mengnan centered on China's tobacco control
policy. As part of Professor Zhang's Gates Foundation–funded initiative, Mengnan
contributed to quantitative policy evaluation work that drew on a multi-source
administrative and survey data platform she helped construct. I co-authored two
papers with her from this work — including a publication in *BMJ Open* (2019, SCI)
evaluating the effects of tobacco control policies on per capita cigarette consumption
in China using monthly data, and a study published in *Health* (2019) on slim
cigarette use and taxation policy. These projects required the ability to manage
large, heterogeneous datasets, apply rigorous statistical methods, and coordinate
across institutional partners — all of which Mengnan handled with the discipline and
precision of a researcher well beyond her years of experience.

I want to speak to one aspect of Mengnan's profile that I am in a particular position
to assess: her capacity to work at the intersection of digital methods and social
science research. My own work — including research undertaken as a Harvard Kennedy
School Asia Fellow on big data and philanthropy, projects commissioned by the
Cyberspace Administration of China, and ongoing research on the digital economy and
developmental social policy — has convinced me that the next generation of governance
scholars must be fluent not only in quantitative research methods but in the
computational infrastructure through which large-scale social processes now leave
documentary traces. Mengnan is that kind of researcher. Her ability to apply
natural language processing and large language model techniques to Chinese governance
documents is not a technical add-on to her scholarly work — it is how she generates
evidence that conventional methods cannot reach. In a 2021 paper co-authored with
Professor Zhang, I argued that developmental social policy is entering a 2.0 era
defined by digital platforms and data-intensive governance. Mengnan's research
agenda is precisely the empirical instantiation of that argument.

CCCW's investment in AI methods for social science, combined with its focus on
Chinese political leadership and governance, positions it as the right institutional
home for Mengnan's work at this stage of her career. The organizational ecology
framework she has developed, grounded in original data infrastructure constructed
under Professor Zhang's supervision, addresses the questions of multi-actor
coordination and state capacity that are central to understanding how China's
governance system actually functions at scale. I am confident that she will contribute
substantively to CCCW's research program — and that the Centre will give her work
the scholarly platform it deserves.

In my years of working with Mengnan, I have found her to be intellectually
self-directed, methodologically rigorous, and collegial in ways that matter for
collaborative research environments. She brings both analytical depth and practical
judgment to her work — qualities that, in my experience, are rarely found together.
I recommend her to your fellowship with full confidence.
